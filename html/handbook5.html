Science and Engineering

NSERC (Science and Engineering Research Canada) is the national instrument for making strategic investments in Canada's capability in science and technology. NSERC supports both basic university research through research grants and project research through partnerships of universities with industry, as well as the advanced training of highly qualified people in both areas.

NSERC "Discovery Grants" are the backbone of Canadian natural science/engineering researchers. NSERC has a New Faculty category that makes it a little easier to achieve success on your first grant application. Otherwise, you are judged on your track record (number and significance of publications) and the proposal you put forth.

Social Sciences and Humanities

SSHRC – The Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council – focuses on funding innovative thinking about real life issues, including the economy, education, health care, the environment, immigration, globalization, language, ethics, peace, security, human rights, law, poverty, mass communication, politics, literature, addiction, pop culture, sexuality, religion, Aboriginal rights, the past, our future.

SSHRC supports a range of funding initiatives. The most important for individual faculty is their Standard Research Grants(SRG) program. These are investigator-driven grants that run up to three years. The idea is to establish a research program, with research projects embedded within. You can then look to a series of grants to cover your long-range work. If you continue to publish at an appropriate rate (and quality), SSHRC committees will look kindly on renewing your funding for the next 3 years.

Researchers may apply for a SRG either as a ‘New Scholar’ or as a ‘Regular Scholar.’ The distinction is made in order to allow new scholars to benefit from a more flexible evaluation of the two main components of an application, the project proposal and the researcher’s curriculum vitae (CV). ‘Regular Scholars’ applications’ are evaluated based on a 40% merit of research proposal and 60% CV. New scholars are evaluated on the
best outcome of a 40-60 or 60-40 evaluation. An applicant is considered a ‘New Scholar’ if at least one of the following applies:

  • Completed highest degree within the five years preceding the date of the
    application submission;
  • Held a tenure-track position for less than five years;
  • Career interruption or delay for family reasons (such as maternity leave);
  • First-time PI on a SSHRC grant
Standard Research Grants
  • Supports programs of research - new or on-going
  • "To support investigator-chosen research in the social sciences and humanities conducted by individuals or teams"

Health Research

Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR) is Canada's major federal funding agency for health research. Its objective is to "excel, according to internationally accepted standards of scientific excellence, in the creation of new knowledge and its translation into improved health for Canadians, more effective health services and products and a strengthened Canadian health care system".

CIHR invests in four "pillars" of health research, including biomedical; clinical science; health systems and services; and the social, cultural and other factors that affect the health of populations.

The CIHR framework includes 13 "virtual" institutes, each one dedicated to a specific area of focus, linking and supporting researchers pursuing common goals.

For an overview of the CIHR, its priorities and opportunities, look through this (2003) Powerpoint presentation by the president, Mark Bisby.

Delegates: The CIHR operates a little differently from the other granting councils. They have a 'University Delegate' program. CIHR keeps them informed of directions, initiatives and decisions, and solicit their active participation on awareness activities. Through them, the CIHR is informed of the views of the community on research opportunities and concerns expressed on CIHR business. Our delegate is Susan Duncan (Dean - Nursing). The ORS has copies of all communiques and delegate meeting minutes.

Canada Foundation for Innovation

The CFI currently funds many of the 'big ticket' projects in Canada. Since 1997 they have approved over 2300 projects representing almost $2 billion. The key word is 'Innovation'. CFI funds infrastructure, not actual research. By creating state-of-the-art infrastructure they want to:

  • transform the way research is done;
  • create a strong and vibrant research environment across Canada;
  • attract and retain excellent researchers;
  • enhance research productivity and the training of highly qualified people;
  • build new national and international networks and partnerships

TRU is eligible for CFI Innovation funding, though smaller universities generally have a tougher time attracting these large grants. To be successful, you must show that your infrastructure will generate world-class research, that it is unique in Canada, and that you have a team that can carry out the work. Competition for CFI funds has become much stiffer in recent years. However, many smaller institutions have been successful by finding a particular niche and exploiting local strengths.

A particularly unexploited funding source for TRU is the Leaders Opportunity Fund - designed to help universities attract and retain the very best of today’s and tomorrow’s researchers at a time of intense international competition. There is no competition for these funds - you simply must prove that you deserve them.

CFI funds only 40% of a project. The remainder typically comes from a mix of provincial government and other sources, usually split 40:40:20.

Leaders Opportunity Fund

    A university may put forward a proposal for a current faculty member who holds a full-time academic appointment, or for an identified candidate whom the university is in the process of recruiting to a full-time academic appointment. Universities are expected to submit LOF proposals to attract or retain faculty in priority areas as identified in their Strategic Research Plan.

    Candidates must be recognized leaders in their field of research, or show promise of becoming research leaders. They must be engaged in, or embarking on, an innovative research program for which the infrastructure is essential and which will provide an enriched research training environment.

NRC-National Research Council

The NRC does a great deal of research, but draws on funds from other granting agencies. It's real strength is in the area of business incubation through the IRAP program.

IRAP - Industrial Research Assistance Program

NRC-IRAP provides Canadian small to medium sized Canadian enterprises (SMEs) with value-added technological and business advice, financial assistance and a range of other innovation assistance. NRC-IRAP helps SMEs realize their full potential, turning knowledge and innovation into strategic opportunities, jobs and prosperity for all Canadians

It offers:

  • Cost-shared R&D funding
  • Repayable financial assistance for projects at the precommercialization stage
  • Youth employment programs

See their website for more details, and be sure to talk to TRU's Advanced Technology Centre staff about your ideas.

 

Canada Council for the Arts

The Canada Council offers a huge range of grants to professional Canadian artists and arts organizations in dance, media arts, music, theatre, writing and publishing, interdisciplinary work and performance art, and the visual arts. All programs are accessible to Aboriginal artists or arts organizations, and artists or arts organizations from diverse cultural or regional communities. Research specific items include:

NRC Partnership: Artist-in-Residence for Research Program
The AIRes program supports research by established independent artists within specific institutes of the NRC. The personal research grants are intended to encourage creativity, collaboration and knowledge transfer between artists and scientific or engineering researchers.

Canada Council for the Arts / Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council New Media Initiative
The Canada Council for the Arts (Canada Council) and the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC) New Media Initiative is intended to promote collaboration linking artists, scientists and/or engineers to combine creativity with the development and application of new technologies and knowledge.

Canada Council for the Arts / Inter-Arts Program
Supports creation, production, dissemination and professional development by Canadian inter-arts professional artists and arts organizations in one of three inter-arts practices: performance art, interdisciplinary work and new artistic practices (including artists and community collaboration activities in one of these three artistic practices).

    • novel approaches to health and safety research
