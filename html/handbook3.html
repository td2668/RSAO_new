3.0 Types of Funding

Faculty projects are funded through two principal avenues - grants and contracts. Different policies and procedures apply to each, so it is important to draw a clear distinction between the two.

Grants

A Research Grant is financial support for an investigator (or investigators) conducting research in a particular subject area or field, usually with the flexibility to pursue new research interests, provided they are within the mandate of the funding agency. A grant has the following characteristics:

  • The research project is designed and controlled by or under the direction of the faculty member;
  • The research project involves no proprietary data protected by the funding agency or any other agency under any contractual commitment to the funding agency;
  • Any patents, inventions, copyrights or other intellectual property belong to the University and not to the funding agency;
  • No direct or indirect reimbursement to the faculty member (except for certain grants from U.S. sources);
  • The funding agency may not restrict publication of the research findings by any approval or delay process;
  • Grant funds are not linked to achieving specific milestones;
  • Grant funds are made available before, not after, expenditures (though this may take place in periodic installments);
  • Capital equipment that is purchased with grant funds belongs to the University and not to the funding agency.
Contracts

A Research Contract is an agreement between two corporate bodies (namely, the sponsor and the University) whereby the sponsor provides financial support for research in a particular subject area or field under specific stipulations and conditions. Contracts usually support a research project.

All contractual agreements referred to in this document are related to research activities. Contracts may include the following types of agreements:

  • Affiliation;
  • Confidentiality;
  • Consulting;
  • Contribution Agreement;
  • License;
  • Materials Transfer;
  • Service/Testing and Analysis;
  • Sponsored Research;
  • Use of Space, Facilities or Equipment.

A Research contract typically possesses many of the following characteristics:

  • The research project is defined by specific stipulations and conditions detailed in the contract, as agreed upon by all parties involved;
  • A set time schedule for the work to be done;
  • Defined deliverables and due dates;
  • Established ownership, patent rights and licensing arrangements;
  • Provisions for confidentiality of information supplied and created by the
    agreement;
  • Established budget approvals and payment schedules;
  • Established considerations for acceptance and/or termination of
    agreement;
  • Limited liability of participants, indemnity;
  • Any other matters that may be appropriate to the circumstances.
Contribution Agreement

A Contribution Agreement lies somewhere between a grant and a contract. Here, the university agrees to make a defined in-kind or other contribution that will complement the sponsor's financial support. The sponsor may be a federal granting agency, for which the agreement has many aspects of a grant. It may also be a private agency, in which the agreement has many aspects of Contract Research. Unlike grants, payment of the financial contribution seldom precedes the conduct of research and invoicing is usually necessary to receive payment. Deliverables are frequently expected.
